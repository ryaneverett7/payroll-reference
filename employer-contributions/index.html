01
The Foundation

What Are Employer Contributions?

An employer contribution is the portion of a benefit's cost that the employer pays on behalf of an employee — separate from what the employee pays themselves through a paycheck deduction. In EN's payroll integration, these are tracked using what are called employer memo codes (also called earning codes) in payroll.

Think of it this way: when an employee enrolls in Medical, there are typically two costs — the employee's share (deducted from their paycheck) and the employer's share (paid by the company). Employee Navigator handles sending the employee's deduction to payroll automatically. Employer contributions are an additional, opt-in layer that tracks the company's side of that cost.

🔑 The Key Distinction

Employee deductions flow from EN to payroll to reduce the employee's gross pay. Employer contributions are memo amounts — they track what the company is spending per pay period on benefits, but they do not come out of the employee's paycheck. They exist in payroll purely for tracking and W-2 reporting purposes.

Why Do Employer Contributions Need to Be in Payroll?

The IRS requires certain employer contributions to appear on the employee's Form W-2. The most significant example is employer-sponsored health coverage — the total cost of the employer's share of health insurance premiums must be reported in Box 12, Code DD on the W-2. Payroll is the system of record for this. EN sends the per-pay-period amounts to payroll so that payroll can accumulate them accurately across the year and report the correct W-2 figure.

Beyond the W-2 requirement, many employers also use these codes to track their HR and benefits spend internally — even for benefit types where IRS reporting isn't mandatory.

💡 Who Is Required to Track This?

Under the Affordable Care Act, employers with 250 or more full-time equivalents (FTEs) are required to report the cost of employer-sponsored health coverage on the W-2 (Box 12, Code DD). For employers under 250 FTEs, this reporting is currently transitional relief — optional but increasingly expected. For other benefit types (Dental, Vision, etc.), employer contribution tracking is generally optional but useful for reporting.

02
Scope

What EN Supports — and What It Doesn't

Employer contribution exchange is currently supported for Paylocity, Paycor, Paychex, and UKG Ready only. Not all benefit types are supported, and some are intentionally excluded due to complexity or partner limitations.

✅ Supported — EN Can Send These

  • ER cost of Medical
  • ER cost of Dental
  • ER cost of Vision
  • ER cost of Hospital Indemnity when the employee pays pre-tax

🚫 Not Supported — EN Will Not Send

  • Employer HSA contributions
  • Employer Group Life contributions
  • Employer Group AD&D contributions
  • Combined Group Life/AD&D plans
  • Employees with imputed income or domestic partners
  • Employer contributions for Commuter benefits
💡 Why Are HSA, Group Life, and AD&D Excluded?

The IRS treats these differently. HSA employer contributions are reported in Box 12, Code W — a separate mechanism with IRS limits and specific tax treatment that payroll systems manage directly. Group Life and AD&D employer contributions involve imputed income calculations (especially for coverage over $50,000) that are too complex for a simple memo code approach. These plan types have the Earning section auto-greyed out in EN and are permanently set to "Never Send."

⚠️ Codes Can Still Be Mapped for Unsupported Types — But No Data Will Send

A broker or HR can map earning codes for unsupported benefit types (HSA, Group Life, etc.) within EN for internal reporting purposes — but EN will not transmit any data to payroll for these. The code sits in the system as a placeholder only. Do not expect payroll to receive or reflect these values.

03
IRS Context

The W-2 Connection — Why This Matters at Tax Time

The reason employer contributions need to flow accurately through payroll all year is simple: payroll is what generates the W-2. If the employer contribution amounts weren't tracked in payroll throughout the year, the W-2 figures will be wrong — and that has IRS consequences.

The Key W-2 Boxes Affected by Employer Contributions

W-2 BoxWhat It ReportsBenefit TypeRequired For
Box 12 · DD Total cost of employer-sponsored health coverage (employer + employee combined premium) Medical / Health 250+ FTE employers
Box 12 · W Employer contributions to an employee's HSA (including employee contributions through a cafeteria plan) HSA All employers with HSA
Box 12 · C Taxable cost of group-term life insurance over $50,000 Group Life When coverage exceeds $50K
Box 1 Wages, tips, other compensation — affected when employer pays employee's share of FICA or provides taxable fringe benefits Various All employers
Box 14a · Other Optional reporting — employer may include contribution amounts here for employee reference (union dues, health premiums deducted, etc.) Various Optional
🔑 Code DD — The Most Important One for EN Customers

Box 12, Code DD reports the total cost of employer-sponsored health coverage — both the employer's share AND the employee's share combined. This is not taxable income, but it must be reported on the W-2 for employers with 250+ FTEs. The value in payroll's DD code accumulates from every pay period's employer contribution transmission. If EN's employer contribution exchange isn't configured and running correctly, this number will be off at year-end, creating a W-2 correction situation (Form W-2c).

What Happens If These Amounts Are Wrong on the W-2?

The IRS treats W-2 errors seriously. Per the 2026 IRS instructions, the penalty for filing an incorrect W-2 is $60–$340 per form depending on how late the correction is made, with maximums up to $4.19M per year for large employers. Errors in employer-reported amounts — especially Code DD — require filing a corrected Form W-2c with the SSA and re-furnishing it to the employee. That's why getting the employer contribution data flowing accurately from EN to payroll all year is far easier than correcting it after the fact.

⚠️ W-2c Is the Fix — But Prevention Is Better

If employer contribution amounts were tracked incorrectly all year and the W-2 is wrong, the employer must file Form W-2c (Corrected Wage and Tax Statement) with the SSA and provide a corrected copy to each affected employee. The IRS only grants one 30-day extension to file W-2s, and only in extraordinary circumstances. Configuring EN's ER contribution exchange at the start of the plan year prevents this entirely.

The Mechanism

How EN Sends Employer Contribution Data to Payroll

EN sends employer contributions the same way it sends employee deductions — as a POST API call to the payroll partner containing the employer memo code and the per-pay-period amount. The key difference is that these are earning codes in payroll, not deduction codes.

Employee Enrolls

Employee completes enrollment in EN. Their per-pay cost is calculated.

EN Calculates ER Cost

EN determines the employer's per-pay-period share based on plan rates and the mapped ER earning code.

EN Sends to Payroll

EN POSTs the ER earning code + per-pay amount to the partner's API. Same transmission as employee deductions.

→
4
Payroll Accumulates

Payroll adds the amount each pay period. Year-end total populates the W-2 (e.g., Code DD for health coverage).

When Does EN Send ER Contributions?

EN sends employer contribution data under the same triggers as employee deductions. Once the ER code is mapped and the plan is set to "Always Send," EN will:

→
Immediately on configuration

When you save the ER code mapping and hit Confirm, EN instantly writes and sends all employer contributions for that plan to payroll for every currently enrolled employee.

→
On new enrollment

When a new employee enrolls in the plan — during New Hire, Open Enrollment, or a Qualifying Life Event — EN sends both the employee deduction and the employer contribution together.

→
When cost changes

If plan rates change (e.g., after OE), EN recalculates and re-sends the updated employer contribution amount just as it does for employee deductions.

→
On termination

When an employee's coverage ends, EN sends a deactivation/end date for the employer contribution record — stopping the accumulation in payroll.

💡 Same RDR Logic as Deductions

Employer contributions follow the exact same Recurring Deduction Record (RDR) logic as employee deductions. They live in the employee's Data Sheet alongside their deduction records. EN only re-sends them when the cost or effective date changes — not on every payroll cycle. The payroll system handles the recurring application on each pay date.

05
Configuration

How to Configure the Exchange of Employer Contributions

Setup is a one-time configuration per plan type. Once done, EN handles the rest automatically. Before starting, confirm with the partner that the ER earning codes are set up correctly in payroll — this must happen in payroll first.

🚫 Critical — HR Must Map These Codes, Not the Broker

A broker should not be mapping ER codes on a customer's behalf. Only HR or the client's payroll admin should map these codes — they are the only ones who truly know what each earning code represents in their payroll system. Incorrect mappings directly impact upcoming payrolls and can be very difficult to undo quickly. If there are questions, direct them to the partner first.

Step-by-Step: Adding an ER Code for Medical

1
Go to Payroll tab → Deduction Codes → View

Select the relevant Payroll Group and click "View Codes" to see the deduction code list for that group.

2
Click "Vendor Payroll Codes" → Refresh Codes

This sends a GET call to the partner's API to pull in all available codes — both deduction and earning codes. This only works for partners that support the Vendor Payroll Codes endpoint (Paylocity, Paycor, Paychex, UKG Ready all do).

3
Click the Edit pencil icon for the Medical plan

This opens the deduction code configuration for that specific plan within the payroll group.

4
Set Transmission Option to "Always Send"

Under Earning Transmission Options, select "Always Send." This tells EN to transmit the employer contribution for every enrolled employee.

5
Set Employer Code to "Yes"

Under Earnings Code → Employer Code, check "Yes." This designates this as an employer-side earning code, not an employee deduction.

6
HR selects the correct ER memo code from the dropdown

HR must choose the earning code that corresponds to their Medical employer contribution in payroll. This is the code that will appear on the W-2 accumulation for Code DD purposes.

7
Save → Review the Confirm prompt → Hit Confirm

EN will display a prompt stating it will sync employer costs to payroll for this plan. By confirming, the customer acknowledges: (a) EN will auto-send all ER costs for enrolled employees, and (b) if the code is later changed or replaced, they are responsible for manually removing costs associated with the original code in payroll.

✅ What Happens Immediately After Confirm

EN instantly writes and sends employer contribution records to payroll for every employee currently enrolled in that plan. You don't need to run a separate job or audit — it fires automatically on save. Future enrollments will also trigger the ER contribution send alongside the employee deduction, with no additional action needed.

Critical Warning

Earning Codes That Must Never Be Mapped Into EN

EN's ER contribution integration is designed for memo tracking codes only — codes that record the employer's contribution amount without affecting net pay calculations. There is a specific set of earning code types in payroll that, if mapped into EN, will cause immediate and serious payroll errors.

🚫 Never Map These Code Types

EN maintains a specific list of ER codes that should never be mapped into the system. These are available in the downloadable file referenced in the EN Help Center article (ER codes that should never be mapped.xlsx). Mapping any of these will negatively impact the client's payroll — specifically by affecting taxable wage calculations, FICA withholding, or other payroll processing logic that payroll manages independently of EN.

The General Rule: Memo Codes Only

The only earning codes that should ever be mapped into EN for employer contributions are pure memo codes — codes that exist solely to track a dollar amount on the employee's pay record without triggering any payroll calculation logic. Ask the HR admin or payroll provider to confirm that the code they're using is a memo/informational code before mapping it.

✅ Safe to Map

  • Memo earning codes used purely to track ER contribution amounts
  • Codes labeled "ERMED," "ERDENT," "ERVIS," or similar informational codes
  • Codes confirmed by HR/payroll admin as non-calculating memo codes

🚫 Never Map

  • Codes that trigger tax calculations or withholding adjustments
  • Codes that affect gross pay or net pay directly
  • Codes used for direct deposit or HSA funding mechanisms
  • Any code the HR admin cannot confirm is a pure memo/informational code
⚠️ If the Wrong Code Is Mapped — Act Fast

Because EN immediately sends ER contributions to payroll upon saving the configuration, a wrong code will begin affecting payroll right away. If an incorrect code is discovered after saving: (1) immediately remove or change the code in EN, (2) contact the partner to manually reverse any amounts that were sent under the wrong code, and (3) confirm the next payroll cycle reflects the corrected amounts. The customer is responsible for cleaning up the original code in payroll — EN cannot reverse what was already sent.

Get the codes right upfront, and the W-2 takes care of itself.
